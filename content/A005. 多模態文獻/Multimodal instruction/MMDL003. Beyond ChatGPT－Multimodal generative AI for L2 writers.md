# Abstract
In response to Warschauer et al.’s paper on the affordances and contradictions of AI-generated text, we as researchers in multimodal literacy focus on discussing the affordances of multimodal generative AI models for L2 writers. These models combine multiple semiotic modes such as **text, images, and audio** to generate coherent and diverse multimodal outputs. For instance, OpenAI’s “DALL⋅E 3 is a text-to-image AI model that creates realistic images and artwork based on language input, and **“Gen-1 ″ is a generative AI model, referred to as “video-to-video”** that uses words and images to produce new videos out of existing videos. Content creators (e.g., L2 writers) can utilize these multimodal generative AI models to produce picture books or videos based on text, image, or video input. Given the potentials of multimodal generative AI models for L2 writers, we discuss their affordances for L2 writers in hopes that this piece can complement Warschauer et al.’s discussion.

# 1. Affordances of multimodal generative AI 
## 1.1. Exploring and fostering multimodal literacy
Multimodal generative AI models provide unique opportunities for L2 writers to explore and foster “multimodal literacy,” which refers to the ability to analyze, interpret, and generate multimodal outputs that integrate various modes (e.g., text, images, videos). While L2 writers interact with multimodal generative AI, they are likely to engage in multimodal literacy by considering the relationships among different modes of communication, deciphering intended meanings, and critically evaluating the effectiveness of multimodal presentations. For instance, when L2 writers need to create a research presentation (e.g., using presentation software like Canva, Prezi, or PowerPoint) about sustainable architecture, they can leverage an image generative AI model, like “DALL⋅E 3 ″ to make their presentation more interactive and engaging. More specifically, if L2 writers input some text of their research findings to DALL⋅E 3, then it can generate a unique visual illustration of sustainable architectural concepts. It attempts to align text descriptions with generated images (read more about DALL⋅E 3 at https://openai.com/dall-e-3 ). Here L2 writers need to examine whether the visual illustration accurately captures the text, and the orchestration of image and text (called a multimodal ensemble) most effectively conveys their intended meanings. If L2 writers find a weak coordination between text and image, they will review, revise, and refine the text, while paying great attention to linguistic mode (text) appropriate to the purpose and audience, until DALL⋅E 3 generates images that are more coordinated with the text. In this process, L2 writers tend to consider coherence between textual and visual elements in multimodal communication, collage text with images, and synthesize information across multiple modes. As such, interacting with multimodal-generative AI affords L2 writers to enhance their awareness of multiple modes and their coordination for meaning-making, to develop multimodal communicative competence (Kang, 2022), as well as to experience iterative and non-linear paths to (multimodal) composing. This is crucial in navigating an increasingly complex and expansive literacy landscape (read Yi, Shin, & Cimasko, 2019 for affordances of multimodal literacy practices for L2 writers).

## 1.2. Developing fine-tuned prompt literacy 
Another affordance of multimodal generative AI is to help L2 writers develop “fine-tuned prompt literacy,” which is related to the first affordance (i.e., fostering multimodal literacy) explained above. The importance of creating and utilizing appropriate AI writing “prompts,” as highlighted in Warshauer et al.’s AI literacy framework, is widely recognized for effectively utilizing text generative AI models like ChatGPT. Expanding on this, we propose the concept of “fine-tuned prompt literacy,” inspired by the term “fine-tuning” commonly used in machine learning. We define fine-tuned prompt literacy as the trained ability or knowledge to appropriately and effectively formulate and adjust prompts, including visual or auditory stimuli, based on specific contexts and purposes. Importantly, active use of multimodal generative AI can assist L2 writers in developing fine-tuned prompt literacy. More specifically, AI prompts tend to include a textual description that specifies the desired output, often accompanied by visual or auditory references. As explained earlier, the multimodal generative AI models use the prompt to generate multimodal outputs that align with the given instructions. L2 writers need to employ and develop their literacy skills and knowledge in order to compose the most appropriate and effective prompt. For instance, L2 writers may initially input a prompt, such as “Illustrate Freedom of the Press.” When they realize this prompt is ineffective to generate an output that captures a complex concept, L2 writers should engage in ‘fine- tuning’ this prompt to clarify an abstract concept by evaluating and reevaluating the relative effectiveness of different prompts with each iteration of output generation. Through the use of multimodal generative AI models, L2 writers engage in the iterative process of fine-tuning a prompt. They may generate a more fine-tuned prompt, like “Show Freedom of the Press with a printing press and diverse newspapers symbolizing information flow.” As such, interacting with multimodal generative AI affords L2 writers to develop fine- tuned prompt literacy.

# 2. Concluding remarks 
As L2 writers increasingly engage in creating multimodal products for various purposes, embracing multimodal generative AI technology seems to be essential in (L2) writing. As discussed in this article, multimodal-generative AI can afford L2 writers to foster multimodal literacy and develop fine-tuned prompt literacy. Participation in multimodal literacy practices holds significant potentials for enhancing language learning, encouraging creative expression, enrich literacy experiences, expanding views of literacy, and expressing identities among L2 writers (Kang, 2022; Yi et al., 2019). In addition, developing fine-tuned prompt literacy is vital to creating optimized AI prompts, which can lead to generating more accurate output. Acknowledging these affordances of multimodal generative AI, we invite L2 writing scholars, professionals, and students to leverage these AI tools for the teaching and learning of L2 writing.
