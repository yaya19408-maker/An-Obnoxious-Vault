49. Aestheteme 美學素
	1. The normative frameworks giving semantic and organizational structure to a world are often discussed as “epistemes”, following Foucault. Less discussed, and of great relevance to us artists, are the corresponding “aesthetemes” that condition perceptibility to the encodings of a world and the way stimuli within them attain a certain semantic legibility.
	2. We need only look to the co-emergence of the concept of Eurohumanist Enlightenment “Man” (episteme) and his particular Renaissance-era space of perspectival projection (aestheteme) to localize his novel self-conception (and ground his space of reasons) in order to understand that abstract concepts are nourished by artifactual externalization for perceptual uptake.
	3. With this imperatively lawlike coherence, a logical corollary follows. This corollary is that in each human societal order, as based on its cosmogonically chartered and genre-specific fictive mode of kind, both Althusser’s “modes of knowledge production” (its episteme), as well as its, so to speak, “aestheteme” – the latter as defined by the archaeologist McNeil as each society’s mode of “representational arts”
50. Etheme 倫理素
	1.  Levi, through his comment and action, endowed his cousin with a distinctive body and made wearing oversized boots into one of the elements of her “ethogram” (Descola 2013: 113) . That unit of behaviour – easily identifiable and distinctive (because susceptible to singling out Nuria’s particular way of being) may metaphorically be dubbed as an “etheme”, by analogy to the linguistic notion of phoneme.

52. Historiographeme 史學史素
	1. historiography 史學史
	2. Unit of historical narrative or analysis
	3. Timothy Langen, in ‘Petersburg as a Historical Novel’ (pp. 102–15) comparesBelyi’s work to Tolstoi’s War and Peace and Scott’s historical novels. He makesastute use of the theories of Lukacs, Andrew Wachtel and Manzoni to point up the importance for the author of historical novels of highlighting the tension between his own creation and historical fact, the response of convincingly realized fictional characters to a march of events they are in no position to understand. This deliberate alienation from the historical context is actually highlighted by authorial insistence on chronologically accurate detail: ‘historiographemes’, ‘the what and when of history’ (p. 107) and even the ‘why’(p. 10)
